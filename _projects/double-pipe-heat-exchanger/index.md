## Overview

This university project began with a straightforward double-pipe heat exchanger in a parallel flow configuration. The baseline design was modelled and analysed in SolidWorks to establish initial performance metrics.

To improve the design, helical fins were introduced around the inner pipe. The fins increased effective surface area, disrupted the thermal boundary layer, promoted turbulent flow, and improved heat transfer while considering fouling, durability, and manufacturability.

## Challenges and Solutions

One major challenge was the discrepancy between experimental and simulation results. External heat losses in the laboratory setup caused measured performance to deviate from simulated values, so the assumptions and data analysis were refined to better account for these losses.

Another challenge was optimising the helical fin geometry. The design needed to balance increased surface area against added flow resistance, requiring iterative simulation and geometry adjustment.

## Results and Performance

The helical fin design significantly improved heat-transfer performance. Simulations showed a 75% increase in heat transfer efficiency compared with the original design. Under identical operating conditions, the helical fin model achieved a heat transfer rate of 1264 W compared with 720 W for the baseline design.

A 3D-printed model was produced to demonstrate the practicality of the design improvements.

## Reflection

This project strengthened my understanding of heat transfer, computational modelling, experimental methodology, and iterative engineering design. It showed how simulation and physical validation can work together to improve a mechanical system.
