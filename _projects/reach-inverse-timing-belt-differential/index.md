## Overview

The wrist joint of the REACH robotic arm was designed using an inverse timing belt differential actuator to achieve compact, efficient, and mechanically balanced motion. The actuator concept was modelled, adapted, and re-engineered in SolidWorks to suit the specific requirements of the REACH arm.

The project emphasised mechanical design and CAD integration. The assembly is currently being fabricated for future prototyping and validation.

## Design Focus

- Parametric CAD modelling of each part in SolidWorks, with attention to tolerance control and assembly fit.
- Complex assembly management across multiple sub-assemblies.
- Motion studies to simulate belt-driven actuation.
- Design adaptation to improve belt routing, integrate with the REACH arm geometry, and support modular mounting.
- Manufacturability planning for 3D-printed components, including print orientation, clearances, and reinforcement.

## Outcomes

Although the actuator has not yet been tested, the design process demonstrates the ability to translate a mechanical concept into a precise CAD assembly, manage a large multi-component system, and adapt an existing mechanism to new physical constraints.

## Future Plans

The next stage is to 3D print and assemble the actuator to validate tolerances, belt tensioning, load handling, motion precision, backlash, and efficiency. Test results will guide further redesign of pulley sizing, belt routing, and structural reinforcement.
