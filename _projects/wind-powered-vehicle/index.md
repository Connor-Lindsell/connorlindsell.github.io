## Overview

As part of a four-member university team, I contributed to the design, construction, and testing of a wind-powered vehicle intended to use wind energy to move against the wind in a wind tunnel. The project applied wind energy conversion theory, including Blade Element Momentum Theory and Betz's Law, to a practical small-scale vehicle.

## Project Goals

The vehicle was designed to transport a payload across a 2-metre track while achieving the highest possible mass/time ratio. The turbine, gear ratio, blade angle, and chassis configuration were iterated to improve performance.

## Key Features

- An 8-blade PVC turbine with adjustable blade angles.
- A string and pulley system that transferred turbine momentum directly to the vehicle axle.
- A lightweight hollow aluminium tube chassis.
- Gear ratio experimentation, with a 1:1 ratio producing the best result.

## Challenges and Solutions

The initial 1:10 gear ratio did not provide enough torque to move the vehicle effectively. After changing to a 1:1 ratio, the vehicle performed much better in testing. Blade angle optimisation was also critical, with 45 degrees giving the best balance between wind energy capture and mechanical output.

## Results

During wind tunnel testing, the vehicle carried a 10.12 kg payload across the 2-metre track in 22 seconds. This produced a payload-to-time ratio of 460 g/s, exceeding the 400 g/s target and making the vehicle one of the stronger performers in its category.
